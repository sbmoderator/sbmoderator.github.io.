Does Islam encourage violence?

Islam does not encourage violence. Instead, it is a religion that values peace and promotes kindness, compassion, and justice. While some people who claim to represent Islam may engage in violent acts, their actions do not reflect the true teachings of Islam. The Quran, the holy book of Islam, promotes nonviolence and forgiveness. The Prophet Muhammad also emphasized treating all people with respect and kindness. Conflict in the Middle East arises from complex factors such as political instability, economic inequality, ethnic and religious tensions, and external interference by foreign powers. It is important to address these factors through peaceful means in order to build a more stable and prosperous region.




Islam Origin

The origin begins with the Messenger of Islam Muhammad (SAW).

Islam is a religion that was founded in the early 7th century by the Prophet Muhammad in the Arabian Peninsula. The teachings of Islam, which were recorded in the Qur'an, focus on the belief in one God, the importance of prayer, fasting, charity, and pilgrimage, and the significance of treating others with respect and compassion. The religion originated in the city of Mecca, where Muhammad was born and raised. He began to receive revelations from Allah through the angel Gabriel, which he began preaching to others, but he faced opposition from the ruling elites who opposed his message. Eventually, Muhammad and his followers migrated to Medina, where he established a community of believers, and continued to spread his teachings. The religion spread rapidly throughout Arabia and beyond, and today it has over 1 billion followers worldwide.




What do Muslims Believe?

Allah (SWT)

Muslims have a unique set of beliefs that shape their worldview and guide their actions. They believe in the existence of one God, Allah, who is the creator of the universe and communicates with humanity through prophets. The final prophet is Muhammad, and his teachings are recorded in the Qur'an, which Muslims believe is the literal word of God. The five pillars of Islam are central to Muslim belief and practice, which includes prayer, fasting, giving to charity, and pilgrimage. Muslims also believe in accountability and judgment in the afterlife, where their deeds during life will determine their fate. They place a high value on family, community, and social justice, striving to treat others with respect and compassion and to make the world a better place through their actions. In essence, the Islamic faith emphasizes submission to the will of Allah and the pursuit of virtuous living.




How Muslims Worship

How do Muslims Worship?

Muslims worship Allah through daily prayer, which involves standing, bowing, and prostrating while reciting verses from the Qur'an. The prayer is performed five times a day and serves as a reminder of the presence of Allah throughout the day. Muslims also give to charity, which is considered a duty and an act of worship. Fasting during the month of Ramadan is another act of worship, which involves abstaining from food and drink from dawn to dusk. It is a time for spiritual reflection, self-discipline, and empathy towards those in need. Muslims also make a pilgrimage to the holy city of Mecca at least once in their lifetime, if financially and physically able, as part of their worship. In addition, Muslims engage in various forms of voluntary worship, such as reading the Qur'an, attending religious gatherings, and performing acts of kindness and service towards others. Overall, worship in Islam encompasses not only formal acts of prayer and ritual, but also a way of life that reflects a deep reverence for Allah and a commitment to virtuous living.




5 Pillars of Islam

Shahadah

1st Pillar

The first pillar is Shahadah—the declaration of belief in Allah as the only God, and of belief in Muhammad as the Messenger of God. This statement of belief affects the whole of a Muslim's life.

Salah (Prayer)

2nd Pillar

Salah, or ritual prayer, is the second pillar. There are five daily prayers, spread at intervals from early in the morning until late at night.

Siam (Fasting)

3rd Pillar

Fasting, called sawm, is the third pillar. Muslims fast every year for the whole of the Muslim month called Ramadan.

Zakat

4th Pillar

The fourth pillar is zakah—the charity tax—which is usually paid annually. Muslims are required to give a small portion of their wealth to support the needy.

Hajj (Pilgrimage)

5th Pillar

The great pilgrimage, called Hajj, is the fifth pillar. Every Muslim wants to make a pilgrimage, or journey, to Mecca at least once in their lifetime. But only about 10 per- cent of all Muslims accomplish this, as many are too poor. While in Mecca the pilgrims participate in many activities and rituals that are meant to show the unity of the Muslim community.
